What's about to change

Six months of skin: a working watch list of regulatory shifts, trial milestones, category consolidations, and marketing pivots — annotated with what each one means for the careful reader.

For the RRF reader, plain

Four threads to follow

  1. Thread 1 · Sunscreen unlocked

    The American sunscreen aisle is finally getting a new filter.

    Bemotrizinol — the workhorse ingredient that makes Korean and French sunscreens cosmetically and photostably better than American ones — is on track for FDA approval in 2026. The first new US sunscreen filter since 1996. Once finalized, expect a wave of US reformulations within 12–18 months. Every "I import my SPF from Seoul" piece of skincare writing becomes a vintage artifact.

  2. Thread 2 · Ozempic face is real

    "Ozempic face" is now a clinical category, not a meme.

    GLP-1 weight-loss drugs are mass-market. The fat-pad atrophy and accelerated facial deflation that follow are the dominant new clinical complaint in aesthetic practices. Filler protocols are being repositioned, and Debut Biotech has launched the first AI-engineered elastin peptide explicitly targeted at the category. This is a new layer on the structural mechanism.

  3. Thread 3 · Grading season

    The luxury "longevity skincare" vocabulary is entering grading season.

    Real human reprogramming trials are starting (Life Biosciences, January 2026). The FDA is escalating exosome enforcement. The senolytic research that "zombie cell creams" were borrowing from is quietly under-delivering. Brands borrowing this language without earning it — see our audit — are about to be exposed.

  4. Thread 4 · Pigmentation shift

    Pigmentation is shifting away from hydroquinone.

    Cysteamine and tranexamic acid are taking share. Our pigmentation map will need to lead with this trio (cysteamine, TXA, azelaic acid) inside a year. The shift is regulatory as much as scientific: hydroquinone's unrestricted-compounding era is closing.

The state of play in May 2026 is that several long-overdue regulatory shifts are landing at once, the longevity-skincare industry is consolidating around a small set of actives, and a few categories that were unfalsifiable a year ago are about to either prove themselves or get publicly graded. This is the watch list — concrete dates where they exist, strong indicators where they don't, and a calibration section at the end on what we're not betting on.

What we know is happening, and when

1. Bemotrizinol clears the FDA

Thread 1 · Sunscreen unlocked

This is the single most consequential near-term event in skincare. Bemotrizinol — the broad-spectrum next-generation UV filter the EU, Asia, and Australia have used for over twenty years — is finally on track for US approval. The chronology, all sourced:

  • November 13, 2025. The SAFE Sunscreen Standards Act was signed into law as part of the OTC Monograph User Fee Act reauthorization. This is the legislative unlock.
  • December 11, 2025. The FDA formally proposed adding bemotrizinol to the OTC sunscreen monograph (FDA News & Events).
  • December 12, 2025. The Federal Register published Amending Over-the-Counter Monograph M020: Sunscreen. Public comment opened.
  • January 26, 2026. Public comment period closed.
  • 2026 (date TBD). Final administrative order expected, after which manufacturers can begin formulating.

Why this matters in plain terms: the United States has not approved a new sunscreen filter since 1996. Bemotrizinol is one of the most photostable, broad-spectrum UVA / UVB filters in the world, and it is the workhorse ingredient that makes Korean, French, and Australian sunscreens cosmetically superior to almost everything sold at an American counter. Once the final order lands, expect a wave of US reformulations within 12–18 months.

What RRF says On SPF: ✓. The substack has covered the sunscreen-filter gap and the Korean / French import workaround clearly and often. The piece worth writing now is the one that gets ahead of the reformulation wave: which US brand puts bemotrizinol in market first, and which existing imports become redundant.
Watch for

Which US brand reformulates first. Likely candidates: Supergoop, EltaMD, La Roche-Posay's US arm. The first head-to-head review of US vs. Korean vs. French sunscreens with the same active will be the year's most-shared skincare piece.

2. The first human partial-reprogramming trial

Thread 3 · Grading season

On January 30, 2026, the FDA cleared Life Biosciences (David Sinclair's company) to begin the first human trial of partial cellular reprogramming via gene therapy, targeting non-arteritic anterior ischemic optic neuropathy (NAION) initially. This is the science the entire luxury "longevity skincare" category has been borrowing vocabulary from. Until now, Yamanaka-style reprogramming was a mouse story plus a Nobel from 2012; as of Q1 2026, it is a human trial in a single tissue type.

Why a skincare reader cares: once a human trial is running, brands will both (a) escalate their reprogramming-adjacent marketing claims and (b) be more exposed when those claims are graded against actual data. The gap between "Augustinus Bader's TFC8 supports stem cell renewal" and "Life Biosciences just got approval to test actual cellular reprogramming, in the eye, in humans, with cancer-risk monitoring" is going to be the year's most useful editorial frame.

Watch for

The first consumer brand to claim "reprogramming-inspired" formulation. Expect it within six months. The test should be: name the transcription factors, name the delivery vehicle, name the tissue endpoint. If the answer is none of the above, it is not reprogramming-inspired — it is vocabulary-inspired.

3. FDA exosome enforcement escalates

Thread 3 · Grading season

The agency issued at least two significant warning letters in 2025 specifically against topical exosome marketing — Supreme Rejuvenation, LLC (May 5, 2025) and Platinum Biologics, LLC (August 15, 2025). Both are searchable in the FDA's Warning Letters database. Industry compliance newsletters (Cosmetics Design, Happi) are tracking it closely. The FDA's position is that human-derived exosomes used therapeutically are unapproved biologics; plant-derived exosomes are in a different regulatory bucket but still subject to MoCRA cosmetic claims rules.

Exosome serums are one of the largest "longevity skincare" categories by 2025 sales and one of the most evidence-thin (see our audit on Parallel Health for the adjacent personalization theater). The macro story is regulatory tightening. The micro story is that we are likely to see at least one major brand pulled or quietly reformulated in 2026, and the marketing language will shift from "human exosomes" to "exosome-inspired" or "plant-derived exosomes" as the legal sands move.

Watch for

Which major luxury brand drops or reformulates its exosome serum first. The reformulation will not be announced. The product will simply change copy on the label and the claims page.

4. The rest of the SAFE Sunscreen pipeline accelerates

Thread 1 · Sunscreen unlocked

The 2025 SAFE Sunscreen Standards Act doesn't just approve bemotrizinol — it modernizes the FDA's framework for future filter approvals. The other long-pending filters now have a faster path: Tinosorb M (bisoctrizole), Uvinul A Plus (diethylamino hydroxybenzoyl hexyl benzoate), and Mexoryl 400 (methoxypropylamino cyclohexenylidene ethoxyethylcyanoacetate, the deepest-UVA filter L'Oréal patented and the EU has used for years).

This is the second-order story behind the bemotrizinol headline: not one new filter, but the procedural unlock for several. Expect filings to accelerate through 2026 and into 2027. For the consumer, this is the difference between "the US has one new filter" and "the US is no longer a generation behind."

Watch for

The first formal filing of either Tinosorb M or Mexoryl 400 under the modernized framework. Either would be a stronger signal than the headline event.

Medium confidence — strong indicators, no firm dates

What we expect, with reservations

5. The Ozempic-face economy reshapes aesthetic dermatology

Thread 2 · Ozempic face is real

GLP-1 weight-loss drugs (semaglutide, tirzepatide) are now mass-market, and "Ozempic face" — gauntness, fat-pad atrophy, accelerated facial deflation — is the dominant new clinical complaint in aesthetic practices. The 2025–2026 wave of dermatology coverage in NewBeauty and peer-reviewed journals confirms this is a sustained category, not a moment. It is, mechanistically, an acceleration of the structural mechanism on the field guide's map.

What's coming over six months: filler protocols specifically marketed for GLP-1 patients (RHA Resilient Hyaluronic Acid is being repositioned), Sculptra and Radiesse as biostimulator alternatives, and the emergence of "GLP-1 skin support" topical routines. Debut Biotech announced an AI-engineered elastin peptide explicitly targeted at Ozempic face — the first specifically-targeted ingredient launch for the category.

Watch for

The first head-to-head trial of fillers in GLP-1 vs. non-GLP-1 patients. Also: clinical-grade elastin restoration as a marketing claim, and whether it survives the first independent audit.

6. Microdose isotretinoin goes mainstream

Microdose isotretinoin (10–20 mg, 1–2× per week) for adult acne and "anti-aging" is the year's clearest TikTok-to-clinic translation. Coverage in 2025–2026 from Cutis ("Patient Buzz: Microdosing Isotretinoin") and major aesthetic-medicine outlets has gone from skeptical to procedural. The FDA loosened some iPLEDGE requirements in 2025 (mostly for males and non-pregnancy-capable patients), reducing access friction.

The protocol is real. The evidence base is small — most of the published work is short-term maintenance studies (Rao et al. on 20 mg / week, small samples). Long-term low-dose RCT data is the thing the field hasn't generated yet, and the regulatory question is whether the "micro" framing leads to off-label normalization that iPLEDGE was designed to prevent.

Watch for

The first significant adverse-event analysis or FDA response to off-label normalization. Also: the first proper RCT on long-term low-dose protocols. Until either lands, microdose is a working clinical practice with weak underlying evidence — not snake oil, but not yet the routine recommendation it's becoming.

7. OneSkin's OS-01 is the canary for the whole category

Thread 3 · Grading season

OneSkin is the most credible existing example of a longevity-mechanism-targeted topical with published human data. The original biopsy work (n≈22) has been extended in 2025–2026 by a 12-week clinical study and a body-lotion arm reporting reduced inflammation markers. The hair-serum arm is getting consumer traction.

Why this is the canary: if OS-01 generates more rigorous third-party RCT evidence in 2026, the entire senescence category gets validated and the rest of the "zombie cell" creams have something concrete to be measured against. If it doesn't — and the category's most legitimate player can't produce stronger data — the whole "senolytic skincare" movement is exposed as having been carried by one company-funded trial.

Watch for

Any independent (non-company-sponsored) RCT of OS-01. That's the single piece of evidence that decides whether senescence-targeted topicals become a real shelf category or remain a high-status anomaly.

8. Cysteamine + tranexamic acid take share from hydroquinone

Thread 4 · Pigmentation shift

Cyspera (Scientis Pharma) relaunched in 2025–2026 with a refreshed Cysteamine Isobionic-Amide Complex line. The pigmentation category is undergoing a quiet but real shift: hydroquinone, the gold-standard tyrosinase inhibitor for fifty years, is increasingly restricted under MoCRA enforcement of compounded products and ongoing concerns about exogenous ochronosis. Cysteamine is the strongest evidence-based replacement, with multiple RCTs showing comparable efficacy without hydroquinone's risk profile.

Alongside cysteamine, tranexamic acid is taking share — topical 5% TXA and oral 250 mg twice daily are now first-line in many derm practices for melasma. Our pigmentation map already lists this trio (cysteamine, TXA, azelaic acid) as the working evidence stack; within a year, it should lead the section.

Watch for

The first major derm-cosmeceutical brand to launch a clinical-grade cysteamine product at non-Cyspera price. Also: any FDA enforcement action against compounding pharmacies still selling unrestricted hydroquinone — that's the regulatory complement to the scientific shift.

Coming — but not on a calendar

The categories worth watching without a date

9. Topical rapamycin is the next "credible-sounding longevity active"

Thread 3 · Grading season

Quoin Pharmaceuticals has been pushing rapamycin formulations through clinical pipelines (port wine stains is the lead indication; aging is the implied long game). At least one prominent 2025–2026 paper, Can a Transplant Drug Safely Slow Human Aging?, and a small University of Wisconsin trial showed measurable epidermal changes from topical rapamycin in older adults.

Rapamycin is, mechanistically, the cleanest mTOR inhibitor in the longevity-research playbook — it is the molecule that connects deregulated nutrient sensing to a real clinical intervention. If a credible cosmetic-grade topical rapamycin launches with real human data, it becomes the first true hallmark-targeted topical with mechanistic legitimacy beyond OS-01.

Watch for

A major derm-cosmeceutical brand (likely SkinCeuticals, SkinMedica, or Allergan's SkinMedica arm) launching a rapamycin product in 2026. The marketing claim to interrogate: does the product cite topical rapamycin RCTs, or does it cite the oral / systemic literature? The latter is the citation-transfer move we flagged in the Timeline Mitopure audit.

10. Senolytic human evidence keeps not showing up

Thread 3 · Grading season

The 2025–2026 review literature is increasingly skeptical. TruDiagnostic published a notable analysis — DNA Methylation Reveals Senolytic Treatments Do Not Reverse Epigenetic Aging — meaning the surrogate biomarker for senescent-cell clearance isn't moving in human trials of dasatinib + quercetin or fisetin protocols. The Mayo Clinic NCT07025226 trial of sequential D + Q + fisetin + temozolomide is recruiting. The 2026 npj Aging review titled From Broad-Spectrum Senolysis to Precision Reprogramming is itself a tell — the field is quietly conceding that the current generation of senolytics aren't the answer.

Why a skincare reader cares: every "senescent cell" / "zombie cell" cream is borrowing legitimacy from a research category that itself is increasingly under-delivering. This is the equivalent of the "stem cell" claim a decade ago, mid-retreat.

Watch for

The first major cosmetic brand quietly deprecating its senolytic claims. The label won't change overnight; the marketing copy and clinical-trial citations on the brand site will. Save screenshots; the rewrite is the story.

11. Injectable peptide stacks are the next aesthetic-medicine concern

Coverage in 2025–2026 of "glow peptide stack injections" — compounded GHK-Cu, BPC-157, TB-500, and other peptides administered subcutaneously by aesthetic clinics, often without strong evidence base or regulatory clarity. Major outlets are starting to cover safety questions. The FDA has placed several peptides on Category 2 of its compounding bulks list (effectively restricted for traditional pharmacy compounding).

Watch for

The first significant adverse-event report or FDA enforcement action against an injectable-peptide clinic. This is the next category likely to get a "lawsuit and headlines" moment in 2026, and it will arrive from an aesthetic clinic operating in a state with weak medical-board oversight.

12. AI-designed ingredient pipeline matures

Thread 2 · Ozempic face is real

Debut Biotech's $20M raise in 2025, plus the broader AI-ingredient discovery wave (covered in 2026's "4 AI Startups Shaping the Future of Beauty" reporting and Fast Company's 2026 Most Innovative list), means the first generation of "designed-not-extracted" peptides and small molecules is hitting consumer products in 2026. Debut's Ozempic-face elastin peptide is the lead example.

This is both a real efficacy story and a real marketing threat. The legitimate version is that algorithmically designed peptides can target specific receptors more precisely than natural-extract peptides. The marketing version is "AI-designed" becoming a meaningless prestige label, the way "stem cell" was in 2014. The grading frame from our audit applies directly: real biology / immature evidence / marketing far ahead / theater.

Watch for

Which AI-designed ingredient gets the first independent peer-reviewed RCT. Until that lands, "AI-designed" is a sourcing claim, not an efficacy claim — and the difference is the entire editorial wedge.

Calibration — what we are not betting on

The trends we're choosing not to credit

To be honest about the watch list, here is what we are deliberately not predicting will have a credibility-creating moment in the next six months. Each is real as a category. None is real as a near-term consumer story.

Microbiome-personalized skincare

Parallel Health, OmieSkin, and similar will keep raising funds and shipping product. The skin microbiome shifts day-to-day with washing, weather, products, and sweat; sampling at one moment and prescribing a custom serum on the result is theater (we've graded one specific instance in the audit). None of it is going to matter clinically in the next six months.

NAD+ / NMN topicals

The oral evidence is weak. The topical evidence is essentially nonexistent. This category will continue to launch with "longevity" claims and will not have a credibility-creating moment in six months.

"Skin longevity" clinics and biological-age testing

The biomarker science (Horvath methylation clocks et al.) is real research. The consumer-facing products built on top of it are mostly wellness theater. The press cycle will continue; the underlying evidence won't.

How this changes our coverage

Three things, beyond the watch list itself.

The sunscreen counter is going to need a dedicated piece soon. Once the bemotrizinol final order lands, the existing US-vs-Korean sunscreen comparisons will go stale fast. The next Field Guide installment was already going to be on sunscreen filters; the timing is now urgent rather than thematic.

The "longevity skincare" grading frame from the audit is the dominant editorial tool of the year. Three of the four threads above (Ozempic face, grading season, pigmentation shift) are about brands borrowing more vocabulary than they've earned, in different categories. The four-grade system — proven / immature / ahead / theater — applies cleanly to each.

Pigmentation needs to be promoted out of the adjacent section. It's currently filed alongside hormonal aging and microvasculature, which understates how much real shelf-shifting is happening. Cysteamine, tranexamic acid, and azelaic acid form a clean intervention trio with its own evidence base; they deserve a Field Guide section of their own.

The skincare aisle is finally moving on the things that matter, mostly because regulators caught up to a 1996 baseline. The luxury vocabulary is moving the other way — toward exposure. Naming which is which is the work.

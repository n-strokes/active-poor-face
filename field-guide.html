The Field Guide · No. 1

What's actually happening to your skin

A working map of the nine cellular mechanisms of skin aging — sorted by which ones you can do something about, which ones you can influence at the margins, and which ones no product or procedure currently reaches.

Working draft · Updated May 9, 2026

Most skincare writing tells you what to buy. This tells you what's happening underneath, so you can decide for yourself.

The framework here comes from a 2023 review in Aging and Disease, "Hallmarks of Skin Aging," which adapts the foundational 2013 paper by López-Otín and colleagues, The Hallmarks of Aging, in Cell. Together they organize everything that happens to a face over decades into nine specific cellular mechanisms. Both are open-access; see the bibliography below.

A face does not age in nine equal ways. Two of the nine do most of the visible work. Three more matter clearly. Four are real, but for a person standing at a Sephora counter in 2026, mostly out of reach. Sorting them honestly is most of the value of this map. Almost everything you can buy — and most of what you can read — sits somewhere in the gap between mechanisms that matter and mechanisms that products can credibly address.

One thing the original nine miss: they are cellular-aging mechanisms. They don't directly cover what most people are reacting to in a mirror — pigmentation, structural change, hormonal shifts at perimenopause, the barrier, the small visible vessels in the cheeks. Those get their own section at the end, marked clearly as adjacent rather than inside the original framework.

Tier 1 — The Two That Matter Most

What does most of the work, visibly

1. Sun damage to DNA

Genomic instability, in the paper. The mechanism, end to end, is short enough to keep in your head: ultraviolet light hits a skin cell. It breaks DNA directly (UVB) and floods the cell with reactive oxygen species (UVA, which passes through windows and reaches you on overcast days). The cell's stress sensors switch on a transcription factor called AP-1. AP-1 turns up the production of two enzymes — MMP-1 (collagenase) and MMP-3 — whose job is to chew up collagen. They do. The collagen scaffolding of your dermis fragments. Over years, that's lines, laxity, mottled tone, and skin cancer.

That single chain — UV → DNA damage and ROS → AP-1 → MMP-1, MMP-3 → collagen fragmentation — is what makes "wear sunscreen" feel like physics instead of nagging. In the dermatological consensus, roughly 80% of facial aging in fair skin is photoaging. Not time. Sun.

What actually intervenes:

  • Daily broad-spectrum SPF, applied generously, reapplied if you're outside. The single highest-leverage thing a person can do for their face. In a 4.5-year Australian trial (Hughes et al., 2013, the Nambour study), the daily-use group showed roughly a quarter less visible photoaging than the as-needed group — and that's before counting the cancers nobody mentions in beauty writing.
  • Topical antioxidants in the morning — vitamin C as L-ascorbic acid, paired with vitamin E and ferulic acid — neutralize some of the reactive damage UV initiates. A partner to sunscreen, not a substitute.
  • Topical retinoids at night (tretinoin, adapalene) inhibit the MMPs that break down collagen after UV exposure, and stimulate new collagen synthesis. Five decades of trials.

The reason that Korean tube of Isntree on your shelf has Tinosorb S and the American one at the Sephora counter doesn't is that the FDA hasn't approved a new sunscreen filter since 1996. American sunscreen lives a generation behind European, Korean, and Japanese sunscreen for entirely regulatory reasons. This is the kind of fact that should change how you shop, not a vague "you should wear SPF."

What RRF says On UV: ✓. Covers SPF rigorously and frequently — the closest the substack gets to consensus dermatology, and right to be there. The mechanism chain underneath the recommendation is the part still worth importing.
What doesn't, despite the marketing

"DNA repair" creams, where the active molecules don't penetrate to where they'd need to act in any meaningful concentration. The entire "blue light protection" category absent UV protection. Anything labeled "anti-aging" without a stated mechanism — that's vocabulary, not chemistry. La Roche-Posay's "ON switch" page is the standing example of cosmeceutical vocabulary-cosplay: a real biological term ("longevity switch") borrowed to dignify a moisturizer with no plausible mechanism for engaging it.

2. Cells that won't die

Cellular senescence, in the paper. The biggest mechanism in modern aging research. As skin cells take damage — from UV, from oxidative stress, from time — some stop dividing. They don't die. They sit there secreting a cocktail of inflammatory signals called the senescence-associated secretory phenotype, or SASP, that damages neighboring cells and accelerates every other mechanism on this list. SASP is the reason aging compounds: a senescent cell isn't just a cell that quit working, it's a cell actively making things worse around it. It's also why a face begins to change visibly faster after about forty — the cells that quit decades ago are now loud.

What actually intervenes:

  • Sun protection, again. UV is the major upstream cause of senescence in skin.
  • Retinoids, which appear to have some senolytic-adjacent effects in skin, though the evidence is still developing.
  • Lifestyle that lowers systemic inflammation: sleep, exercise, alcohol moderation, smoking cessation. These reach senescent cell burden indirectly but reach it.
What RRF says On senescence: silent. The SASP is the most powerful single concept in modern skin-aging science, and it's missing from the substack's vocabulary. This is the gap most worth closing — it's also the one your reader will most enjoy filling in, because the term flatters.
What's coming, but isn't here yet

True senolytics — drugs that selectively kill senescent cells — are an active frontier of aging research and may eventually reach dermatology. They are not in any product on a shelf, regardless of marketing claims. Anything currently selling itself as a "cellular reset" or "rejuvenation" is borrowing the vocabulary of the science without delivering its mechanism.

Tier 2 — The Three That Matter Clearly

Real, visible, and partly within reach

3. Sugar damage to collagen

Grouped under loss of proteostasis in the paper, but it deserves its own line because the mechanism is unusually clean and unusually neglected. Sugar molecules in your blood — glucose, fructose — bind to long-lived proteins like dermal collagen and form advanced glycation end-products, or AGEs. These cross-link collagen fibers, making them stiff, brittle, and yellow. The result, over decades: a sallower, less elastic, less responsive skin texture that no topical reverses.

This is irreversible at the molecular level. You can't un-glycate collagen — only replace it, which the body does more slowly with each decade. People with poorly controlled diabetes show visibly accelerated skin aging precisely because they have higher glycation rates.

What intervenes: a lower-glycemic diet, less sugar, less refined carbohydrate. Decade horizon. Boring. Free.

What RRF says On glycation: nearly silent. The "no red wine for collagen" line gestures at it, but the AGEs mechanism — the one your reader has already read about in longevity content — never gets named. This is a sellable insight virtually no skincare writer reaches for, and it's right at the intersection of skin and the metabolic-health vocabulary the audience already speaks.
What doesn't

"Anti-glycation" creams. No topical has been shown to reverse existing AGE crosslinks in living dermal collagen. A few ingredients (carnosine, alpha-lipoic acid) have in vitro data on glycation precursors. None of this translates to a product you can apply tonight to undo what twenty years of soda did.

4. Inflammaging

Chronic, low-grade inflammation throughout the body. The paper places this in the antagonistic-responses bucket — defenses that have stopped working properly. Senescent cells contribute, but so do sleep deprivation, chronic stress, alcohol, smoking, gut dysfunction, and visceral fat. The same MMPs UV activates are activated by general inflammation, so a tired, stressed face takes the same kind of collagen hit a sun-exposed face does — quieter, slower, but ongoing.

What intervenes: sleep, exercise, alcohol moderation, smoking cessation. Smoking cessation in particular is the single most cost-effective skin intervention available — measurable improvement in tone within months. Topical niacinamide and azelaic acid have modest local anti-inflammatory effects and are cheap.

What doesn't, mostly

"Calming," "soothing," and "balancing" product lines, generally built around plant extracts with weak clinical evidence. The category isn't fake — sensitive skin is real — but most of the products treat the local symptom, not the systemic inflammation actually driving most of the visible damage.

5. Structural and scaffolding loss (adjacent)

Not strictly one of the nine cellular hallmarks — this lives in the bone, the fat pads, the ligaments, the muscles, not in the cells of the skin itself. But it is what most people are reacting to when they say their face has "changed." The skull resorbs over decades. Fat pads atrophy and migrate downward. Expression muscles hypertrophy. Ligaments laxen. The face deflates and slides; the skin sitting on top of it follows.

No topical addresses this. Nothing you put on your skin replaces lost bone or fat. This is the mechanism that procedures exist to address, and the one that explains why, past a certain age, topicals alone produce diminishing visible returns regardless of how good they are.

What intervenes:

  • Hyaluronic acid filler — mechanical volume replacement. Real, well-evidenced short-term, with underdiscussed risks: vascular events, migration, and recent imaging studies (Humphrey et al., 2023) suggesting filler may persist in tissue longer than manufacturers claim, sometimes years past its supposed dissolution.
  • Sculptra and similar biostimulators — induce the body to lay down new collagen, slowly, over months. A different mechanism from filler.
  • Botulinum toxin — prevents the repeated muscle folding that etches dynamic wrinkles into static lines. Clean mechanism, strongest evidence base in cosmetic dermatology.
  • Surgery, when the structural change is severe enough that no injectable can keep up.
What RRF says On structural intervention: ✓ but uncritical. Filler in particular gets enthusiastic coverage and not enough on the imaging-study question of how long it actually stays in the face. The 2023 Humphrey paper deserves a sentence the substack hasn't given it.
What doesn't

Every cream marketed as "lifting." Topicals do not lift faces. They can improve the quality of the skin sitting on top of a structure, but they cannot reshape the structure underneath. This is also the mechanism behind most "instant lift" claims, which are temporary tightening from film-formers, not lifting in any anatomical sense.

Tier 3 — The Four You Can't Really Touch

Real science, no consumer interventions yet

The remaining four mechanisms in the framework are well-validated in the laboratory and have essentially no current consumer-side intervention. The vocabulary from this part of the science has been borrowed especially heavily by luxury skincare marketing, because it sounds impressive and is hard to falsify. If a product claims to address any of the following four, the claim is almost certainly running ahead of the evidence.

6. Telomere attrition

Chromosome ends shorten with each cell division. Past a threshold, cells stop dividing. Heritable, mostly determined by genetics and overall systemic health. No topical or cosmetic procedure addresses telomere length.

7. Cellular drift

Epigenetic alterations, in the paper. The chemical marks that tell each cell what kind of cell to be drift over time. Skin cells, in effect, "forget" what they're supposed to do. One of the hottest areas in aging research; cellular reprogramming therapies are years from clinical use and longer still from your bathroom shelf.

8. Stem cell exhaustion

The pool of basal-layer stem cells that replenish skin gradually depletes. Wound healing slows. Skin thins. There is no cream that meaningfully restores this pool, regardless of "stem cell" labeling. Plant stem cell extracts, despite popular marketing, do not transmit any cell-renewal signal to human skin.

9. Deregulated nutrient sensing

The mTOR, insulin, and AMPK pathways stop responding properly. This is what caloric restriction and rapamycin research are about. Real, but at the systemic level, and not a skincare problem in any direct sense.

What RRF says · 6–9 The luxury vocabulary the substack occasionally repeats — "cellular renewal," "stem-cell-derived," "longevity-coded" — is borrowed almost entirely from this tier, where no product can credibly act. The single most useful editorial move available is to tag the borrowing every time it appears.

Adjacent — Outside the original nine

What the framework misses, but you see in a mirror

The López-Otín nine are cellular-aging mechanisms. They don't directly cover several of the most visible things on a face. These belong on the map, marked clearly as adjacent rather than inside the original framework — and they're where some of the highest-leverage and lowest-cost interventions actually live.

Pigmentation

The original nine treat pigment as a downstream consequence of UV damage. It is — but for a large share of the audience, particularly the deeper-skin-tone reader the substack explicitly addresses, pigment is the primary concern, not a side effect. The pathway has its own vocabulary: tyrosinase as the rate-limiting enzyme, melanocyte hyperactivity, melanosome transfer, dermal pigment that has dropped past the basement membrane and become very hard to remove. Hyperpigmentation, melasma, post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH), and lentigines are different conditions with different best-evidence interventions.

What intervenes (with the matching condition):

  • SPF, always, because all four conditions are driven or worsened by UV.
  • Tranexamic acid — oral and topical — for melasma. Strong recent evidence, still under-used.
  • Cysteamine for stubborn melasma and PIH where hydroquinone has failed or isn't available.
  • Azelaic acid for melasma, PIH, and rosacea overlap. Cheap, well-tolerated, evidence-graded.
  • Hydroquinone, prescription, short courses only.

Hormonal aging

Absent from the López-Otín framework, but the most predictable and dramatic skin event in the average woman's life. Estrogen withdrawal at perimenopause accelerates collagen loss roughly 30% in the first five years post-menopause. Sebum production drops; the skin gets drier and less elastic on a fast timeline that has nothing to do with sun exposure or sleep.

What intervenes:

  • Systemic HRT, when clinically indicated. A legitimate skincare conversation now in a way it wasn't ten years ago.
  • Topical estriol creams — legal in the EU, off-label and compounded in the US — with growing evidence for facial collagen.
  • The same Tier 1 stack (SPF, retinoid, vitamin C) does heavier lifting in this window because the skin's intrinsic repair capacity has dropped.

The barrier (and the microbiome)

The stratum corneum — the outermost dead-cell layer — is held together by a lipid mortar of ceramides, cholesterol, and free fatty acids. When that mortar thins, water leaves the skin (transepidermal water loss), irritants get in, the barrier gets reactive, and every active ingredient you apply works less well and stings more. This is not in the original nine, and it is where most of the cheap, evidence-based wins live.

What intervenes:

  • A simple barrier-supporting moisturizer with ceramides and niacinamide. CeraVe and Vanicream do this for under $20.
  • Avoiding over-exfoliation, which is the most common self-inflicted barrier injury.
  • Petrolatum at night when the barrier is acutely impaired ("slugging"). Boring. Effective.

The microbiome marketing claims are mostly downstream of this — barrier health and microbiome health track each other. You don't need to dignify the prebiotic-serum vocabulary to get the result; supporting the barrier carries the microbiome along.

Microvasculature

Capillary fragility, telangiectasias (visible broken vessels), the diffuse redness of late-stage rosacea, and post-inflammatory erythema after acne — these are vascular changes, not collagen ones, and they don't fit cleanly into any of the nine. They have their own intervention map: vascular lasers (KTP, pulsed-dye), niacinamide, azelaic acid, and the prescription rosacea ladder.

The environmental layer: pollution, IRA, blue light

Three contributors to visible aging the original framework doesn't separate cleanly:

  • PM2.5 — fine particulate pollution — has been correlated with lentigines and wrinkles in epidemiological studies. The mechanism is partly oxidative, partly inflammatory. SkinCeuticals' urban-pollution serum line points at a real signal even when the product itself is marginal.
  • Infrared (IRA) radiation penetrates deeper than UV and contributes to photoaging through different mechanisms. Iron oxides in tinted sunscreen address it; clear chemical sunscreens don't.
  • Visible / high-energy blue light (HEV) contributes to pigmentation in deeper skin tones (melasma in particular). The brand "blue light protection from your phone" claims are commercially overstated, but the mechanism is real for sunlight, and iron oxides cover it.

The practical move is simple: a tinted mineral sunscreen with iron oxides addresses UV, IRA, and HEV in a single product.

What this means for what you actually do

Once you understand the map, the entire skincare aisle simplifies. Most products and procedures aim at mechanisms 1, 2, 3, 4, or 5, often without saying which. The honest version of a daily routine, ranked by mechanism and impact:

The non-negotiables — mechanisms 1, 2, partly 4

  • Sunscreen every morning, generously applied, reapplied if outside for hours. Tinted mineral if you also want IRA and HEV coverage.
  • A topical retinoid at night, used consistently over years.

The high-leverage adds — mechanisms 1, 2, 4, barrier

  • Vitamin C with vitamin E and ferulic acid in the morning, especially if sun damage is your concern.
  • A simple barrier-supporting moisturizer (ceramides plus niacinamide). Doesn't itself reverse aging — stops you sabotaging the rest.

Targeted, only if you have the matching concern — pigment, vascular, hormonal

  • Tranexamic acid or cysteamine for stubborn pigmentation. Azelaic acid for redness, melasma, or mild acne overlap.
  • Benzoyl peroxide and prescription retinoids for active acne.
  • Topical estriol or systemic HRT in the perimenopausal window — clinical conversation, not Sephora.

The lifestyle layer — mechanisms 2, 3, 4

  • Don't smoke.
  • Sleep enough.
  • Limit alcohol.
  • Watch your sugar load over decades.

The procedure layer — mechanisms 5, 2

  • Botox for dynamic wrinkles, when they begin to set.
  • Filler for volume loss, with eyes open about the tradeoffs and the imaging-study question.
  • Microneedling and energy-based devices for collagen induction — modest effect sizes, variable evidence by device.
  • Vascular lasers for telangiectasias and persistent redness.

The everything else — the $300 cream, the LED mask of the moment, the ten-step routine, the exosome serum, the ingredient that wasn't there last year — is either marketed against a mechanism it doesn't reach, or doing something the items above already do for less.

That's the map. The rest of this site is the work of testing specific products and procedures against it.

Glossary — key terms used above
AGEs
Advanced glycation end-products. Sugar–protein adducts that cross-link collagen and elastin over decades. Driver of mechanism 3.
AP-1
Activator Protein 1. A transcription factor switched on by UV-induced stress; turns up the production of MMPs.
Fibroblast
The dermal cell that makes collagen, elastin, and the extracellular matrix that gives skin its structure.
HEV
High-energy visible light, sometimes called "blue light." Contributes to pigmentation in deeper skin tones.
IRA
Infrared-A radiation. Penetrates deeper than UV, contributes to photoaging through partly distinct mechanisms.
MMP-1, MMP-3
Matrix metalloproteinases — collagen-degrading enzymes activated by AP-1 after UV exposure.
Photoaging
The portion of visible facial aging attributable to UV exposure rather than chronological time. Roughly 80% in fair skin.
PIH
Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation. Pigment left after acne, eczema, or other inflammation resolves.
Retinoid
Vitamin A derivatives (tretinoin, adapalene, retinaldehyde, retinol) that down-regulate MMPs and stimulate collagen synthesis.
ROS
Reactive oxygen species. Unstable, damaging oxygen molecules generated by UV exposure and metabolic stress.
SASP
Senescence-associated secretory phenotype. The inflammatory cocktail secreted by senescent cells. Driver of mechanism 2.
Senolytic
A drug that selectively kills senescent cells. Active research area; nothing on a consumer shelf.
Tyrosinase
The rate-limiting enzyme in melanin synthesis. Target of most pigment-modulating actives.

# What's Coming in the Next Six Months

The state of play in May 2026 is that several long-overdue regulatory shifts are landing at once, the longevity-skincare industry is consolidating around a small set of actives, and a few categories that were unfalsifiable a year ago are about to either prove themselves or get publicly graded. Here is what the careful reader should be watching, with concrete dates and sources.

## High-Confidence Inflections With Real Dates

### 1. Bemotrizinol (Tinosorb S) FDA approval — pending finalization in 2026

This is the single most consequential near-term event in skincare. Bemotrizinol — the broad-spectrum next-generation UV filter that the EU, Asia, and Australia have used for over twenty years — is finally on track for US approval. The chronology, all sourced:

- **November 13, 2025**: SAFE Sunscreen Standards Act signed into law as part of the OTC Monograph User Fee Act reauthorization. This is the legislative unlock. (Source: PersonalCareInsights, June 2025; multiple confirmations in late 2025.)
- **December 11, 2025**: FDA press announcement formally proposing bemotrizinol be added to the OTC sunscreen monograph. (Source: FDA News & Events.)
- **December 12, 2025**: Federal Register publishes "Amending Over-the-Counter Monograph M020: Sunscreen." Public comment period opens.
- **January 26, 2026**: Public comment period closes.
- **2026 (date TBD)**: Final administrative order expected, after which manufacturers can begin formulating with it. (Source: Holland & Knight legal analysis; ScienceDirect, Wang et al., 2026, "Pending FDA Approval of Bemotrizinol: A Potential Turning Point for U.S. Sunscreen Formulation.")

Why this matters in plain terms: the US has not approved a new sunscreen filter since 1996. Bemotrizinol is one of the most photostable, broad-spectrum UVA/UVB filters in the world, and it is the workhorse ingredient that makes Korean, French, and Australian sunscreens cosmetically superior. Once finalized, expect a wave of US reformulations within 12–18 months. Every "I order my sunscreen from Korea" piece of skincare writing will become a vintage artifact. Watch for: which US brand reformulates first (likely candidates: Supergoop, EltaMD, La Roche-Posay's US arm).

### 2. Life Biosciences first human partial-reprogramming trial — IND cleared January 30, 2026

The FDA cleared Life Biosciences (David Sinclair's company) to begin the first human trial of partial cellular reprogramming via gene therapy, targeting non-arteritic anterior ischemic optic neuropathy (NAION) initially. This is a real regulatory milestone — the kind of thing the Monia Substack article waved at as 2030s science fiction but is actually happening now in the eye. (Source: NAD.com, January 30, 2026.)

Why a skincare reader cares: this is the science the entire luxury "longevity skincare" category has been borrowing vocabulary from. Once a human trial is running, brands will both (a) escalate their reprogramming-adjacent marketing claims, and (b) be increasingly exposed when those claims are graded against actual data. The gap between "Augustinus Bader's TFC8 supports stem cell renewal" and "Life Biosciences just got approval to test actual cellular reprogramming in a single tissue type" is going to be the year's most useful editorial frame. Watch for: the first consumer brand to claim "reprogramming-inspired" formulation. Expect it within six months.

### 3. FDA exosome enforcement is escalating

The agency issued at least two warning letters in 2025 specifically against topical exosome marketing — Supreme Rejuvenation, LLC (May 5, 2025) and Platinum Biologics, LLC (August 15, 2025). Industry compliance newsletters are tracking it closely (Cosmetics Design, Happi). The FDA's position is that human-derived exosomes used therapeutically are unapproved biologics; plant-derived exosomes are in a different regulatory bucket but still subject to MoCRA cosmetic claims rules.

Why this matters: exosome serums are one of the largest "longevity skincare" categories by 2025 sales and one of the most evidence-thin. The regulatory tightening is the macro story. The micro story is that we're likely to see at least one major brand pulled or reformulated in 2026, and the marketing language will quietly shift from "human exosomes" to "exosome-inspired" or "plant-derived exosomes." Watch for: which major luxury brand drops its exosome serum first.

### 4. Bemotrizinol's regulatory arrival also unlocks the rest of the SAFE Sunscreen pipeline

The 2025 SAFE Sunscreen Standards Act doesn't just approve bemotrizinol; it modernizes the FDA's framework for *future* filter approvals. This means the other long-pending filters — especially Tinosorb M (bisoctrizole), Uvinul A Plus (diethylamino hydroxybenzoyl hexyl benzoate), and Mexoryl 400 (methoxypropylamino cyclohexenylidene ethoxyethylcyanoacetate) — now have a faster path. Expect filings to accelerate. (Source: SAFE Sunscreen Standards Act analysis, Personal Care Insights and US Sunscreen Reform reporting, late 2025.)

## Medium-Confidence Inflections With Strong Indicators

### 5. The Ozempic-face economy reshapes aesthetic dermatology

GLP-1 weight loss drugs (semaglutide, tirzepatide) are now mass market, and "Ozempic face" — gauntness, fat-pad atrophy, accelerated facial deflation — is the dominant new clinical complaint in aesthetic practices. The 2025–2026 wave of dermatology coverage confirms this is a sustained category, not a moment. (Sources: NewBeauty 2026 reporting, multiple peer-reviewed PMC articles in 2025–2026 on rising GLP-1 use and skin concerns.)

What to watch over six months: filler protocols specifically marketed for GLP-1 patients (RHA Resilient Hyaluronic Acid filler is being repositioned), Sculptra and Radiesse as biostimulator alternatives, and the emergence of "GLP-1 skin support" topical routines. **Debut Biotech announced an AI-engineered elastin peptide explicitly targeted at Ozempic face** (cosmeticsdesign coverage, 2025–2026). This is the first specifically-targeted ingredient launch for the category. Watch for: clinical-grade elastin restoration as a marketing claim, and the first head-to-head trial of fillers in GLP-1 versus non-GLP-1 patients.

### 6. The Hollywood Dose / microdose isotretinoin goes mainstream

Microdose isotretinoin (10–20mg, 1–2x/week) for adult acne and "anti-aging" is the year's clearest TikTok-to-clinic translation. Coverage in 2025–2026 from *Cutis* ("Patient Buzz: Microdosing Isotretinoin") and major aesthetic-medicine outlets. The FDA loosened some iPLEDGE requirements in 2025 (mostly for males / non-pregnancy-capable patients), reducing access friction. (Sources: multiple dermatology trade publications, late 2025.)

What to watch: the first proper RCT data on long-term low-dose protocols (the existing literature is small-sample maintenance studies). Also, the regulatory question — the FDA is watching whether the "micro" framing leads to off-label normalization that the iPLEDGE program was designed to prevent. Expect coverage of the first significant adverse-event analysis.

### 7. OneSkin's OS-01 peptide is the canary for evidence-based longevity skincare

OneSkin published a 12-week clinical study in 2025 expanding the human-data footprint for OS-01 beyond the original n=22 biopsy work. The brand's body lotion arm published "Novel OS-01 Peptide Body Lotion Reduces Inflammation" data. The hair serum arm is getting consumer traction. (Sources: OneSkin company communications, dermatology reviews 2026.)

Why this is the canary: OS-01 is the most credible existing example of a longevity-mechanism-targeted topical with published human data. If it generates more rigorous third-party RCT evidence in 2026, the entire category gets validated. If it doesn't, and the category's most legitimate player can't produce stronger data, the whole "senolytic skincare" movement is exposed. Watch for: any independent (non-company-sponsored) RCT of OS-01.

### 8. Cysteamine takes share from hydroquinone in pigment treatment

Cyspera (Scientis Pharma) relaunched in 2025–2026 with a refreshed Cysteamine Isobionic-Amide Complex line. The pigmentation category is undergoing a quiet but real shift: hydroquinone, the gold-standard tyrosinase inhibitor for fifty years, is increasingly restricted under MoCRA enforcement of compounded products and consumer concerns about ochronosis. Cysteamine is the strongest evidence-based replacement, with multiple RCTs showing comparable efficacy without hydroquinone's risks. (Sources: Cyspera product communications, dermatology reviews 2026, "Melasma: A New Era of Topical Treatment Options Galore.")

Watch for: tranexamic acid taking share alongside cysteamine. Topical 5% TXA and oral 250mg twice daily are now first-line in many derm practices for melasma. The Field Guide's pigmentation section is going to need to lead with this trio (cysteamine, tranexamic acid, azelaic acid) within a year.

## The Things to Watch That Aren't Dated But Are Coming

### 9. Topical rapamycin is the next "credible-sounding longevity active"

Quoin Pharmaceuticals has been pushing rapamycin formulations through clinical pipelines (port wine stains is the lead indication; aging is the implied long game). At least one prominent 2025–2026 paper, "Can a Transplant Drug Safely Slow Human Aging?", and a small University of Wisconsin trial showed measurable epidermal changes from topical rapamycin in older adults. (Source: NewBeauty / dermatology trade coverage.)

Why this matters: rapamycin is mechanistically the cleanest mTOR inhibitor in the longevity research playbook. If a credible cosmetic-grade topical rapamycin launches with real human data, it becomes the first true "longevity hallmark"–targeted topical with mechanistic legitimacy beyond OS-01. Watch for: a major dermatologic-cosmeceutical brand (likely SkinCeuticals, SkinMedica, or Allergan/AbbVie's SkinMedica arm) launching a rapamycin product in 2026.

### 10. Senolytics human evidence keeps not showing up

The 2025–2026 review literature is increasingly skeptical. TruDiagnostic published a notable analysis: "DNA Methylation Reveals Senolytic Treatments Do Not Reverse Epigenetic Aging" — meaning the surrogate biomarker for senescent-cell clearance isn't moving in human trials of dasatinib + quercetin or fisetin protocols. The Mayo Clinic NCT07025226 trial of sequential D+Q + fisetin + temozolomide is recruiting. The Nature *npj Aging* 2026 review titled "From Broad-Spectrum Senolysis to Precision Reprogramming" is itself a tell — the field is quietly conceding that current senolytics aren't the answer.

Why a skincare reader cares: every "senescent cell" / "zombie cell" cream is borrowing legitimacy from a research category that itself is increasingly under-delivering. Watch for: the first major cosmetic brand quietly deprecating its senolytic claims. This will be the equivalent of the silent retreat from "stem cell" claims a decade ago.

### 11. Injectable peptide stacks are the next aesthetic medicine concern

Coverage in 2025–2026 of "glow peptide stack injections" — compounded GHK-Cu, BPC-157, TB-500, and other peptides administered subcutaneously by aesthetic clinics, often without strong evidence base or regulatory clarity. Major outlets are starting to cover safety questions. The FDA has placed some peptides on Category 2 of its compounding bulks list (effectively restricted). (Source: aesthetic medicine and compounding pharmacy news, 2025–2026.)

What to watch: the first significant adverse-event report or FDA enforcement action against an injectable-peptide clinic. This is the next category likely to get a "lawsuit and headlines" moment in 2026.

### 12. AI-designed ingredient pipeline matures

Debut Biotech's $20M raise (2025) plus the broader AI-ingredient discovery wave (covered in 2026's "4 AI Startups Shaping the Future of Beauty" reporting and *Fast Company*'s 2026 Most Innovative list) means the first generation of "designed-not-extracted" peptides and small molecules is hitting consumer products in 2026. Debut's Ozempic-face elastin peptide is the lead example.

Why this matters: it's both a real efficacy story and a real marketing threat. The legitimate version is that algorithmically-designed peptides can target specific receptors more precisely than natural-extract peptides. The marketing version is "AI-designed" becoming a meaningless prestige label, the way "stem cell" was in 2014. Watch for: which AI-designed ingredient gets the first independent peer-reviewed RCT.

## A Few Things I'd Suggest the Article *Not* Predict

I want to be calibrated. Some things you'll see breathlessly covered in skincare press over the next six months that I would not bet on:

The microbiome-personalized-skincare boom continues to be unsupported by clinical evidence. Parallel Health, OmieSkin, and similar will keep raising funds and shipping product. None of it is going to matter clinically in the next six months. Don't let the article credit the category.

NAD+ / NMN topicals will continue to launch with "longevity" claims. The oral evidence is weak; the topical evidence is essentially nonexistent. This category will not have a credibility-creating moment in six months.

"Skin longevity clinics" and biological-age testing will get press. The biomarker science (Horvath clocks etc.) is real research; the consumer products are mostly wellness theater. Don't extrapolate.

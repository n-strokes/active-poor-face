# The Skincare Landscape According to RRF

A rigorous mapping of every product, ingredient, and treatment Sade has named — sorted by what the evidence actually supports, what's plausible-but-overhyped, and what's bordering on snake oil. This is the working draft; we'll refine each tier as we research deeper.

---

## Tier 1: Things with real, well-replicated clinical evidence

These are the load-bearing items. If RRF is right about anything, it's these — and notably, they're also among the cheapest.

- **Prescription tretinoin** (Altreno, Arazlo, generic 0.025–0.1%). The single most-studied topical in dermatology. ~50 years of randomized trials showing increased epidermal turnover, dermal collagen synthesis, fading of photoaging and hyperpigmentation. Sade is correct that this is the highest-leverage product in her stack, and correct that the co-pay route via "treating acne" is how most people actually access it cheaply. Where she's slightly off: Altreno (lotion) and Arazlo (lotion, tazarotene) aren't "fancier" — they're better-tolerated vehicles, which matters for adherence, not potency. Generic tretinoin cream at 0.025% does the same biology for ~$15 with GoodRx. The "fancy" framing is brand marketing she's absorbed. Adapalene 0.1% (Differin, OTC, $13) has near-equivalent data and is what dermatologists actually start most patients on now.
- **Daily broad-spectrum SPF.** Robust evidence — the Australian Nambour trial (Hughes et al., 2013) showed daily SPF use slowed visible photoaging by ~24% over 4.5 years vs. discretionary use. She's right that this protects every other investment. Where the SPF article gets interesting and where we should pressure-test: the specific products she ranks. Mineral vs. chemical efficacy is roughly equivalent at matched SPF when actually applied at 2 mg/cm². The real-world problem is application thickness, not filter chemistry.
- **In-office microneedling with a medical device** (e.g., SkinPen, Morpheus8, Potenza RF). Decent evidence for fine lines, acne scars, and texture (multiple RCTs, modest effect sizes). The "Superjection" she raves about is essentially branded microneedling delivery — likely effective but not magic.
- **Botulinum toxin** (Botox, Dysport, etc.) for dynamic wrinkles and the "lift." Well-established. Her observation that "glass skin" on Instagram is mostly Botox + good lighting + light-reflecting wrinkle-free surfaces is genuinely correct and under-discussed.

## Tier 2: Plausible, reasonable, but oversold or context-dependent

- **Niacinamide.** Real data for barrier support, mild brightening, sebum modulation at 4–5%. Becomes filler at higher concentrations. It's in nearly every product she lists — fine, but no product deserves credit for "containing niacinamide."
- **Hyaluronic acid (topical).** Hydrates the stratum corneum. The "8 molecular weights" claim from Isntree is a marketing escalation — there's some logic to varied MW penetration, but no head-to-head trial proving 8 weights beats 2. Diminishing returns past ~3.
- **Hyaluronic acid filler** (Restylane, Juvederm, RHA, etc.). Effective for volume, well-studied. The risks she doesn't mention: filler migration, persistent low-grade inflammation, the "pillow face" cascade as you keep chasing balance. There's now real concern in dermatology about HA filler not fully resorbing on MRI even years later (Humphrey et al., 2023). Worth covering.
- **PRP** (platelet-rich plasma). Modest evidence for hair loss, weaker evidence for skin rejuvenation. Heterogeneous protocols make studies hard to compare. The hype-to-evidence ratio is high. Sade saying her skin "has never been glowier" three weeks post-PRP is exactly the kind of n=1 testimonial that makes this category hard to evaluate honestly. PFRM (platelet-fibrin-rich matrix) is even less studied — basically PRP 2.0 marketing.
- **Sculptra** (poly-L-lactic acid). Real biostimulator with real collagen induction data, but operator-dependent and with a non-trivial nodule rate if injected poorly. Worth doing, worth being picky about who.
- **Azelaic acid / Azeloglycine.** Genuinely useful for melanin synthesis inhibition (tyrosinase pathway). Mesoestetic Melan 130 actually has decent clinical backing for post-procedure pigmentation. This one I'd defend.
- **Tinosorb S, Tinosorb M, Uvinul A Plus.** She's right that these are superior next-gen filters with better UVA coverage and photostability than what the FDA has approved. The FDA gap is real (PASS Act has been stuck since 2020). Importing K-beauty / EU sunscreens with these filters is rationally defensible.

## Tier 3: Where I'd push back hard

- **Biologique Recherche P50 1970.** "Jesus in a Bottle" is fantastic copy and bad science. The phenol formulation was banned in the EU because phenol is dermally absorbed and systemically toxic at sufficient exposure — not because European regulators are squeamish. The "no one knows why it works" framing is a tell. We do know: it's a high-acid, high-irritation chemical peel masquerading as a toner. Skin looks better short-term because you're chemically exfoliating; long-term inflammation costs are real. The fact that she's still recommending people hunt it down on eBay post-reformulation is, frankly, irresponsible. This is the closest thing to snake oil in her canon.
- **Eighth Day Skin and Dr. Barbara Sturm.** Both are luxury-tier brands trading heavily on physician-founder mystique. Sturm in particular — the "MC1 cream," the blood-derived products, the $300 hyaluronic serum — has minimal independent clinical data for the price differential vs. CeraVe + niacinamide + a sunscreen. Sturm has been repeatedly flagged by dermatologists (Dr. Michelle Wong / Lab Muffin, Dr. Shereene Idriss) as overpriced relative to formulation. This is the heart of the "buying her own bullshit" hypothesis.
- **Dr. Lara Devgan's product line (provided free).** Conflict of interest is the issue, not the products per se. Anything a brand gives a journalist for free, with that journalist publicly endorsing it, needs an asterisk. The recommendation may still be honest — it's just not independent.
- **Benev exosomes.** Exosomes are a hot category with theoretical promise (cell-cell signaling vesicles) but the FDA explicitly does not recognize topical exosomes as evidence-based, and in 2023 issued a warning letter campaign against exosome marketing claims. Sterility, sourcing, and regulatory status are all unsettled. The "glow" is real because microneedling alone produces glow. The exosome contribution is unproven.
- **"Liquid nose jobs" / non-surgical rhinoplasty.** Real, but the risk profile is genuinely scary — filler near the dorsal/lateral nasal arteries has caused vascular occlusion and blindness. This is one of the highest-risk filler placements. Devgan is good; the procedure category as a casual "drop-in" is misleadingly framed.
- **"You'll always look the age you started tret."** Charming, false. Tret slows photoaging trajectory; it doesn't freeze biological age. The chronological aging mechanisms (intrinsic collagen loss, fat pad migration, bone resorption) proceed regardless.
- **Pico, Potenza, SoftWave stacking for hyperpigmentation in deeper skin tones.** Pico lasers (PicoSure, PicoWay) have data, but PIH risk in Fitzpatrick IV–VI skin from any laser is non-trivial. Stacking three modalities on a patient already dealing with a hyperpigmentation flare is aggressive. I'd want to see how she does at 6 months.
- **Microdosed Accutane.** Trending on TikTok, basically zero peer-reviewed data on long-term low-dose protocols outside of specific adult acne contexts (Rao et al. studied 20mg/week for maintenance — small samples). Not snake oil, but the "all the celebs do it" framing is exactly the social-proof reasoning we should be skeptical of.

## Tier 4: Things conspicuously absent (and where the real money / efficacy is)

This is the most interesting list. These are the things with strong evidence that she hasn't mentioned at all — meaning either she doesn't know, doesn't care, or her network doesn't push them. Each one is a legitimate "things RRF doesn't know" angle for our site.

- **Topical antioxidants** — specifically L-ascorbic acid 10–20% + ferulic + vitamin E (the SkinCeuticals C E Ferulic stack). This is the single most-cited adjunct to SPF in dermatology, with the original Pinnell/Duke patents and replicated efficacy data. Not mentioned once.
- **Bakuchiol, granactive retinoid, retinaldehyde** as tret alternatives for pregnancy / sensitivity. Absent.
- **Sunscreen reapplication.** She covers SPF selection but the meta-question — how do you reapply over makeup at hour 4? — is the actual unsolved problem. Powders (ColoreScience Sunforgettable), sticks, Supergoop (Re)setting Mist. Not addressed.
- **Polypodium leucotomos (Heliocare)** oral photoprotection. Modest but real evidence as adjunct. Cheap. Absent.
- **Tranexamic acid** — topical and oral. Strong emerging evidence for melasma and PIH, increasingly first-line in dermatology for pigmentation. Absent. Given she's actively battling hyperpigmentation, this is a notable omission.
- **Cysteamine (Cyspera).** Genuinely effective hydroquinone alternative with growing data. Absent.
- **Energy-based collagen induction beyond microneedling** — Ulthera (microfocused ultrasound), Sofwave, Thermage FLX, the new Sylfirm X. SoftWave is mentioned in passing but not contextualized.
- **Exosome alternatives** — growth factor serums with actual data (TNS Recovery Complex, SkinMedica HA5). Mid-tier evidence, often more rigorous than exosomes.
- **Sleep, glycation / AGEs, and dietary glycemic load** as collagen variables. Zero mention. Probably the highest ROI lifestyle lever after SPF.
- **Photobiomodulation** (red light, 630–660nm, near-IR 830nm). Omnilux Contour, Dr. Dennis Gross mask, Celluma. Genuine RCT evidence for collagen and wound healing. Absent — and a conspicuous gap given the at-home device boom.
- **The "sunscreen filter audit"** — pulling apart which legacy US filters (oxybenzone, octinoxate) are systemically absorbed per the FDA's 2019/2020 MUsT studies. This is the actual scientific story behind why next-gen filters matter, and it's bigger than "Korea has cooler SPF."

## The snake-oil-vs-truth investment thesis

If I'm steelmanning your hypothesis — yes, I think she's buying into a meaningful amount of bullshit, particularly around: P50, exosomes, the luxury skincare brands provided free by publicists, and the implicit assumption that more procedures stacked = better. But she's clearly right about the load-bearing trio (tret, SPF, Botox-for-lift) and clearly thoughtful about practitioner selection.

The website opportunity is to be the rigorous overlay on RRF's taste. She has the access, the network, and the voice. What's missing is a layer that says: here's the evidence grade for each thing she recommends, here's what she doesn't know exists, and here's where you can save 90% by buying the active ingredient instead of the brand story.
